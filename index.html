The hitting was not the problem. The league got 486 hits in 838 at-bats — a raw .580, right about what it has been doing all year. Then it caused 38 outs, and the week's real number became .535.

Thirty-eight is a lot. There have been 136 caused outs all season, and 28% of them arrived in 21% of the at-bats. The rate went from .031 per at-bat to .045 — a 46% spike, and the entire difference between an ordinary week and the coldest one this paper has recorded. Nothing was wrong with the swings. The problem was what happened after them.

Only 59 of the 121 players who batted beat the league's own rate for the week. The other 62 are the reason it was .535.

Adjusted average = (H − CO) / AB, verified exact on all 144 rows. z-scores computed within each draft round (n = 12 per round), so treat individual z values as directional, not gospel. Sleeper and outlier tables use minimum at-bat filters (15 and 10 respectively) to keep small samples from stealing the show. The week = games 9 and 10, the two games every club played between the July 3 and July 10 snapshots, joined on (team, draft pick); the week's rate = (ΔH − ΔCO) ÷ ΔAB. The swing that drives the Collapse, the Surge and the Team Temperature bars = that rate minus the player's or club's own season average at the July 3 snapshot — deliberately not the change in the season average, which is the same signal multiplied by ΔAB ÷ total AB (0.21 this edition, and shrinking every week). Perfect weeks and week swings require 4+ period at-bats — period at-bats ran 3 to 17 this week, median 7, and nobody took one or two — while the Records Board keeps its stricter 10+ definition. A week is written as a line ("7-for-7"): the hits are raw, the average is net of caused outs, which is why a 2-for-5 week with two caused outs is worth .000. Season-average movers require 8+ ABs on July 3 and 15+ on July 10.
